Playing Twenty Questions with ChatGPT

Author: Parth Parikh

First Published: 17/03/23

It is better to have an approximate answer to the right question than an exact answer to the wrong one.
 - John Tukey

About a year and a half ago, I had the opportunity to take an NLP class at NC State taught by Prof. Munindar Singh. The class was absolutely fascinating, and it led me to discover more about the classical side of NLP.

One of my favorite discussions in his class was about the Centering Theory. This framework from the 90s was used, among many things, to model coherence in a text. The idea is to start by dividing the text into utterances, or sentences for simplicity, and identifying the center, which would be the most salient part of an utterance (usually an entity). Once we have the center, we can identify how it moves in future utterances. If it bounces around too much, we can say that the text is less coherent. The second edition of Jurafsky and Martin's Speech and Language Processing book has a nice section on this topic, Section 18.1 on page 685.

We also had some other interesting discussions, such as about languages that are not written (e.g., Konkani), whether there are any true synonyms, how French has a more prominent gender classification, and how the French government tries to regulate their language.

Anyway, in his course, we were tasked with brainstorming and working on an NLP project. My colleague Anisha and I decided to work on reversing the twenty-questions game.

Reversing the Twenty Questions game

So, what is this game all about?

Some of you may have heard of the twenty-questions game or a similar online version called Akinator. The game involves a questioner and an answerer. The answerer thinks of a random person, such as a famous movie star like Tom Cruise. The objective of the game is for the questioner to guess this person in less than twenty questions by asking a series of yes-no type questions to the answerer. For instance, is the person male? Is he a movie star? Has he acted in any superhero films? And so on.

Today, efficient computer programs like Akinator can act as the questioner to try and guess the entity we are thinking about. They usually use space-partitioning methods to select questions (based on previously answered questions) that are likely to drastically decrease the search space.

However, we were trying to solve a reverse version of this problem:

Make the computer randomly choose a name and let the human guess the name by asking the computer a series of natural language questions. The game ends when the human correctly guesses the name selected by the computer.

In our research, we developed an entire NLP pipeline that included an entity formulation and pronoun resolution phase, a paragraph/sentence retrieval phase, and a boolean question-answering phase. Initially, we encountered some difficulty when working with completely random entities, so we decided to limit the domain to commonly known animals. Although our results were not incredibly promising, they were not underwhelming either.

If you are interested in our research, you can find our paper on Arxiv.org or view our slides.

Anyways, I had given up on this variant of the game until today I decided to play it with ChatGPT. To my surprise, the results were absolutely mind-boggling!
